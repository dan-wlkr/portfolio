Case study Currently

Building a Social Media App

Designing a social platform built around the present moment — minimal by intent, low-friction by design, and entirely free of the algorithmic noise that makes most social apps exhausting to use.

Project Currently
Year 2024
Role Product Designer
Tools Figma, Principle

The idea

Currently started with a simple frustration: every social app I opened showed me things people had done — polished photos from holidays that ended weeks ago, achievements curated for maximum engagement, content optimised for the algorithm rather than the audience. None of it felt honest or present.

The concept for Currently was the inverse: share only what you're doing right now. No editing, no retrospective posting, no performance. Just a lightweight, honest answer to "what are you up to?"

Design constraints

I set strict constraints from the start to keep the product focused:

No editing after posting

Posts are ephemeral and honest. Once you've shared what you're doing, that's it. No going back to refine the caption.

One post at a time

You can only have one active post. Posting again automatically replaces your current status, which keeps the feed genuinely current.

No likes, no counts

Reactions exist but aren't counted publicly. This removes the incentive to optimise for engagement and reduces social comparison.

Chronological only

The feed is strictly chronological. No algorithm, no promoted content. What's at the top is simply what's most recent.

Process

I started with a positioning exercise — mapping the space between "professional" (LinkedIn), "aspirational" (Instagram), and "real-time" (Twitter/X) to find where Currently could live. The gap was clear: a calm, present-tense space for close connections, not broadcasts.

"The posting experience had to feel effortless. If it took more than three taps to share something, it was too much friction for a moment that's already passed."

I prototyped heavily in Figma and ran informal tests with a group of friends over two weeks — having them use the prototype as if it were a real app, then debrief on what felt natural and what broke the flow. The posting sheet went through nine iterations before the gesture-based dismiss felt right.

Visually I leaned into a minimal, text-forward aesthetic. The feed is type-led with optional images — because most "currently" moments don't require a photo, and forcing a camera-centric model would change the kind of content people feel comfortable sharing.

Key screens

The core flow covers three moments: posting, browsing the feed, and viewing a friend's full status. I kept the navigation to a single bottom bar with three items — home feed, search, and your profile. Anything that didn't serve those three moments was cut.

Onboarding was designed to get users to their first post within 60 seconds. Rather than a feature tour, it drops you straight into a "what are you doing right now?" prompt — one text field, one optional image, one button. The product explains itself through use.

Reflections

Currently remains a personal project — it hasn't been built for production. But it taught me a lot about designing systems with intent: every constraint I set had a downstream effect on the experience, and holding those constraints under pressure (when it would have been easy to add "just one more feature") was the real design challenge.

The project also reinforced for me that good mobile UX isn't just about the interface — it's about the model underneath. The decisions about what users can and can't do shaped the product's personality more than any visual choice I made.
