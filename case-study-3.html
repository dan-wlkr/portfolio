The project

Gridfinite started as a conversation with my son about what makes a board game fun to play together on a device — specifically the kind of game you pass between two people on a single phone, taking turns on the same screen.

Most pass-and-play games we found were either too simple (noughts and crosses) or too complex for a casual session. We wanted something with just enough strategy to be interesting, but simple enough to pick up in under two minutes without reading a manual.

Game design first

Before touching Figma, we spent several evenings at the kitchen table sketching mechanics on paper and playing them with coins and a grid drawn in biro. This was the most valuable part of the process — it forced us to stress-test rules quickly without worrying about how anything looked.

"The best rule change came from my son noticing a loophole we'd missed after about 90 seconds of play. Paper prototyping is brutally efficient."

The core mechanic that survived: players take turns placing tiles on a grid, scoring points for completing rows and columns. Tiles have different point values and some have special effects. The grid is fixed at 6×6, which gives enough space for interesting play without games running long.

Designing for pass-and-play

Pass-and-play introduces a specific UX challenge that most app design ignores: the screen physically rotates between players. This meant designing every view to work at 180° rotation, and making the transition between turns feel intentional rather than disorienting.

I designed a "hand-off" screen — a full-bleed overlay that confirms the previous player's score and prompts the next player to tap to reveal the board. This creates a natural moment to physically pass the device, prevents peeking, and adds a small ritual that makes the turn structure feel satisfying.

Colour was important: each player has a distinct accent colour used for their tiles, score, and the hand-off screen. Both colours needed to work at the same brightness level to feel fair — neither player's colour should feel "better" than the other's.

Building in Swift

My son handled the majority of the Swift/SwiftUI implementation, with me advising on structure and working through bugs together. It was a genuine collaboration — he'd build something, I'd suggest a UX change, he'd push back if it was technically painful, and we'd find a middle ground.

We kept the codebase small and readable, which made it easier to work on together. The game state is managed with a simple model that both of us could reason about easily, which matters when you're debugging together at 9pm.

What I took from it

  • 01 Designing for a physical handoff is a genuinely different problem space from standard mobile UX — the screen orientation, the "sealed" hand-off moment, and the shared device context all need deliberate design decisions.
  • 02 Paper prototyping game mechanics is orders of magnitude faster than digital prototyping. The friction of implementation hides bad ideas; paper forces them to surface immediately.
  • 03 Collaborating with a non-designer on an implementation teaches you where your design decisions create real engineering pain — a perspective I find genuinely useful when working with development teams professionally.
  • 04 Fun is a design requirement. The moments of delight — the tile-drop animation, the score fanfare, the colour of your opponent's defeat screen — matter as much as the rules that make the game fair.
