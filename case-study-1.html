Modernising Internal Operations at Scale

Establishing a design function from scratch at Next, working across six product sets supporting the delivery of up to one million packages a day.

Company Next PLC
Duration 3 Years
Role UX Designer — Internal Applications
Scope Enterprise UX, Design Systems, Automation Interfaces

Overview

When I joined Next, internal product design was a new function within the business. I was brought in to establish design practice from the ground up, working across operational tools that support the delivery of up to one million packages every day. Over three years I worked across six product sets and their respective development teams, designing complex internal applications while building the research practice, tooling, and design infrastructure to support the function long term.

The challenge

Next's internal applications had grown organically alongside the business, resulting in fragmented interfaces that weren't keeping pace with increasingly automated logistics processes. Teams were working with tools that couldn't adequately represent the complexity of modern fulfilment operations, and there was no shared design language to bring consistency across products.

As the first dedicated designer in this space, I had to build credibility, establish process, and ship meaningful work simultaneously.

Understanding the users

Research was central to how I worked across every product set. Over three years I spoke with more than 100 users through moderated sessions, building up a substantial body of qualitative insight across different operational roles and contexts. Observing how staff actually interacted with tools in practice was particularly important — operational users are domain experts, and the gap between what they describe and what they actually do tends to be significant.

To make that research sustainable and cumulative rather than something rebuilt from scratch for each project, I created a structured user database capturing roles, workflows, pain points, and session history. This gave the team persistent institutional knowledge, made it easier to recruit the right participants for future studies, and shifted research from ad hoc conversations to something more systematic and repeatable.

The design work

The six product sets I worked across covered genuinely varied ground, each with its own user base, operational context, and design challenges.

Management dashboards required translating high-volume, fast-moving operational data into interfaces that gave managers a clear picture of what was happening across the business at any given moment. The challenge was less about information architecture in the abstract and more about understanding what decisions these users actually needed to make, and designing around those rather than around the data itself.

Payment processing applications served back office teams handling financial workflows where accuracy and auditability were critical — supporting users who were highly experienced with their processes while reducing the risk of error in high-stakes tasks.

The most complex work was designing applications for Next's new E3 warehouse — a large-scale automated facility where staff weren't doing manual work, but operating alongside automation. Sometimes monitoring and intervening in automated processes, sometimes setting plans and parameters for the system to follow. Designing for this kind of human-automation relationship required thinking carefully about how to represent system state, how to make automated decisions legible, and how to give users meaningful control without overwhelming them with complexity.

I prototyped extensively throughout, using Figma and Figma Make to simulate automated states, edge cases, and handoff moments between human intent and system execution. This was essential for aligning engineers and stakeholders on behaviours that were difficult to describe in static specs, and for pressure-testing designs before any development investment was made.

Building a design system in Blazor

Working across six product sets made the lack of a shared design language a constant friction point. I took it upon myself to solve this, building a design system from scratch — working solo in VS Code with GitHub Copilot to construct it in Blazor, chosen to align with Next's existing tech stack and ensure components could be adopted directly by development teams without translation overhead.

Building it with Copilot was a deliberate bet on AI-assisted development. It allowed me to move at a pace that wouldn't have been realistic working alone in a traditional way, and gave me a much deeper understanding of how components would behave in code, which made handover to engineering teams significantly smoother.

Once the foundation was in place, I scaled adoption by forming a dedicated design system team of 10 developers drawn from across the six product sets. Having existing relationships across those teams was invaluable — creating internal champions in each area, distributing ownership, and ensuring the system was shaped by the people implementing it rather than imposed on them.

To extend the system's reach further, I built a component playground — a tool that allowed non-technical stakeholders like Product Owners to mock up ideas using real components without needing Figma or engineering support. This reduced back-and-forth in early ideation and embedded design thinking earlier in the product lifecycle.

Championing AI across the design team

Beyond my own practice, I actively pushed AI adoption more broadly within the team. I ran presentations on how to integrate AI tools into design workflows — covering practical usage, where the tools add genuine value, and how to think critically about their outputs.

This was part of a wider belief that AI literacy is becoming a core design skill. Getting the team comfortable with these tools early and helping them develop good instincts around when and how to use them felt like one of the more important contributions I could make in a senior capacity.

Impact

Across six product sets, I delivered design work ranging from data-dense management dashboards to payment tooling to genuinely novel interfaces for semi-automated warehouse operations. The design system gave those products a shared visual and interaction language for the first time, reducing overhead and raising the quality baseline across the board. The component playground extended that value to non-technical stakeholders, making the system something the whole product organisation could engage with.

Reflections

Building a design function from scratch inside a large retailer taught me that the work is never just the interface. It is earning trust, creating infrastructure, and bringing technical teams along with you. The E3 warehouse work sits with me as the most stretching design challenge — not because of its technical complexity but because it required developing new ways of thinking about the relationship between human decision-making and automated execution.

Building the design system solo before handing it to a team of ten is probably the thing that best captures how I approach problems: start scrappy, validate fast, then build the structure that makes it last.
