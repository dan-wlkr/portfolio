Case study Next PLC

Modernising Warehouse Operations at Scale

Redesigning internal tooling for Next PLC's warehouse teams to reduce manual effort, surface critical information faster, and scale gracefully as order volumes grew.

Client Next PLC
Year 2023–2024
Role Lead Product Designer
Tools Figma, FigJam

Overview

Next PLC operates one of the UK's most complex warehouse networks, processing hundreds of thousands of shipments a day across multiple sites. When I joined, their internal tooling was a patchwork of legacy systems — green-screen terminals, disconnected spreadsheets, and printed manifests passed between shifts.

There was no product design function. Decisions were driven by the engineering team working from brief verbal requirements, which led to interfaces that technically worked but were poorly suited to the pace and pressure of the warehouse floor.

The challenge

Warehouse workers operate under constant time pressure in a physically demanding environment — they need information at a glance, not buried in menus. Error rates were climbing as volumes grew, supervisors had limited visibility into real-time throughput, and onboarding new staff took far longer than it should.

"Staff were spending 20–30% of their shift navigating the system rather than doing the job. We needed to flip that ratio."

I was asked to define and build a UX practice from scratch: establish research and design processes, audit the existing systems, and deliver a redesigned tooling suite that could be rolled out across the network.

Discovery & research

I spent the first two weeks on the warehouse floor — shadowing pickers, pack-station operators, and shift supervisors across two sites. This was essential: the context of physical work, noise levels, gloved hands on touch screens, and the pressure of throughput targets simply can't be understood from a desk.

From that fieldwork I synthesised six core jobs-to-be-done, mapped the key failure points in existing workflows, and ran a workshop with operational managers to prioritise by impact and feasibility. Two areas emerged as the highest-leverage opportunities: the pick-list interface used by floor staff, and the supervisor dashboard used to monitor site throughput.

Design process

I worked in two-week sprints, testing lo-fi prototypes on the warehouse floor between rounds. I kept fidelity low for as long as possible — paper prototypes are easy to dismiss, which forces honest feedback.

Key decisions included moving to a single-action-per-screen pattern for the pick-list interface, using large tap targets optimised for gloved hands, and building a persistent status bar that gives supervisors live throughput at a glance without requiring them to navigate away from their current task.

I also introduced a component library in Figma for the first time at Next, which allowed engineering to move faster and reduced inconsistency across screens. Working closely with the front-end team, we established a shared language for components that bridged design intent and implementation.

Outcomes

The redesigned pick-list interface launched across two sites before the peak trading period. Supervisor adoption of the new dashboard was near-immediate — the old system was retired within three weeks of go-live.
